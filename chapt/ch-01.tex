\chapter{The UNGA's Role, Reconceptualized} %% {{{
    %% end header ----------------------------

Since Hamas undertook its \enquote{Al-Aqsa Flood} project on October 7, 2023, a corollary project designed to destroy Israel's reputation in the West has been undertaken by multitudes.\footcite[In this paper, the \enquote{West} is meant to connote western European and other societies whose influence and power expanded transcontinentally due to the successes of their eighteenth and nineteenth century era of imperialisms. In recent decades, these societies have often been characterized as having imposed their geopolitical will on \enquote{eastern} societies that were inadequately prepared to defend themselves against Western military, industrial and cultural aggressions, which enabled their long successful practice of \enquote{Orientalism.} In Edward Said's words, \enquote{what the early Orientalist achieved, and what the non-Orientalist in the West exploited, was a reduced model of the Orient suitable for the prevailing, dominant culture and its theoretical (and hard after the theoretical, the practical) exigencies.}][153]{Said-1978a} Built on familiar narratives of the country's extreme racism, leading to its construction and maintenance of an apartheid system in territories that are deemed under international law to be occupied by it, the issue has seeped into the consciousness of activists, scholars, leaders, media figures and everyday people as an affront to humanity. A resulting challenge that now confronts World Jewry --- and not just Israelis --- is how to assess a concomitant resurgence in what is often described as the world's \enquote{oldest hatred,} antisemitism. Thus, while Israel can claim recent victories in the military conflicts ignited by its most passionate and lethal enemies, it must be acknowledged that on a global basis, its defeat in the \enquote{war of words} \parencite{Qutami-2020} raises larger questions for a singular people whose presence is nearly worldwide. 

It is worth revisiting the question of Israel, racism and apartheid now, from a multilayered perspective, to examine it in the context of a larger history of ideas, because much has changed since the current century's first decade. That was when it became clear that Israel's relationship with  Palestinian Arabs would remain fraught with turmoil indefinitely. This people, who have long defined themselves by \enquote{both land dispossession and the 1948 Nakba (Catastrophe), which was accompanied by ethnic cleansing} \parencite[xiii]{Zureik-2016}, has become at the fulcrum of what has become one of the defining controversies of the modern era. Recognizing that their projected history has cast a harshly negative light on Israel, as well as Jews outside Israel who support it, concerned organizations in the Jewish world have long recognized the challenge of the battle of hearts and minds over their past experience and present situation.\footnote{E.g., Michael Herzog, ``Delegitimization: Attitudes Toward Israel and The Jewish People,'' The Jewish People Policy Institute, \url{https://jppi.org.il/uploads/DELEGITIMIZATION-\%20ATTITUDES\%20TOWARD\%20ISRAEL\%20AND\%20THE\%20JEWISH\%20PEOPLE.pdf}. (``The phenomenon of delegitimization and contending with it is a type of asymmetric warfare, but this time conducted on the battlefield of ideas.'')} The issue gained resumed urgency with the waning of the Oslo Accords peace process, which had brought to an end a more quiescent period in organized Israel hatred during the 1990s. It was followed by the violence of the ``Second (or Al-Aqsa) Intifada'' from 2000 to 2005, the taking by Hamas of full control of Gaza after a landslide victory in local elections in 2006.\footnote{Anna Mier y Teran, \enquote{War In Palestine and Its Impact on Western Countries,} Universidad de Navarra Global Affairs and Strategic Studies, January 20, 2024. \url{https://www.unav.edu/web/global-affairs/war-in-palestine-and-its-impact-on-western-countries}.} The increasing anathema and change in tone by Israel's enemies was alarming even before then, but it was not considered entirely unmanageable; that is, accusations of racism, settler-colonialism, ethnic cleansing, apartheid and much else were hardly new, and arguably could be seen as unlikely to jeopardize the long-term trajectory of Israel's economic, societal and cultural progress \parencite[15--26]{Adelman-2008}.  

The violence that has steadily risen against Jewish communities as a result of more recent events --- particularly in the aftermath of October 7, 2023 --- has understandably caused considerable concern. An old era appears to have returned anew, but in its latest incarnation, the ``Zionist'' label offers a convenient disguise. That is, in the popular imagination, while the popular Nazi slogan that \enquote{the Jews are our misfortune}\footnote{In Nazi German, the phrase "Die Juden sind unser Unglück!" was a notorious antisemitic slogan.} is no longer extant, but it is not altogether repudiated, as the term \enquote{Jews} is replaced with \enquote{Zionists}.\footnote{Johannes Due Enstad (2024). ``Facing antisemitism in Europe: individual and country-level predictors of Jews' victimization and fear across twelve countries.'' \textit{Social Forces}, 103(3), 1186--1209. \url{https://doi.org/10.1093/sf/soae091}.}  The now successfully globalized intifada, or at least one that is in a state of effervescent vitality, thus has roots that can be clearly traced to the 2010s,\footnote{A call-to-action was made by the International Union of Muslim Scholars (IUMS), a group led since its 2004 inception by Sheikh Yusuf Al-Qaradawi, ``a leading  Islamic jurist whose pronouncements the Muslim Brotherhood consider authoritative.'' K Shideler and D. Daoud, ``Command and Control: The International Union of Muslim Scholars, The Muslim Brotherhood, and The Call for Global Intifada during Operation Protective Edge,'' Center for Security Policy Occasional Paper Series.  \url{https://www.centerforsecuritypolicy.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/08/Command_and_Control_08-21-14_12.50pm.pdf}. The IUMS made the call to both the entirety of the Muslim \textit{Ummah} and ``free peoples of the world,'' urging them to ``rise up in support of Palestine.''} but the rising tide clearly has much deeper roots. The challenge here, apart from the increasing urgency for defensive measures to protect soft targets from rising intimidation and violence,is to understand these roots better. This leads to a deeper inquiry into the origin and nature of the racism and apartheid narratives hurled so pervasively and incessantly against Israel. In undertaking one, it should be obvious that the question is not whether they are ``true'' in any empirical sense, but whether --- because they are planted, poisonous, and poised to grow, and will continue to threaten all who are openly sympathetic toward or supportive of Israel  --- there are ways to counter the threats they pose from a more insightful perspective. 

This book examines this broad question from one angle in particular: the manner in which the United Nations, and more precisely its General Assembly, or \enquote{UNGA,} has become both a breeding ground and a distribution mechanism for anti-Zionist narratives, and their normalization as political (and therefore protected) speech --- but which, according to UNGA representatives, has nothing to do with antisemitism.\footnote{Alex King, {The United Nations Is Not Anti-Semitic, Its Point Man on Anti-Semitism Says,} B'nai B'rith International, February 14, 2023. \url{https://www.bnaibrith.org/the-united-nations-is-not-antisemitic-its-point-man-on-antisemitism-says/.}} Its core argument is that over the past half century, the distinction has become all but meaningless, primarily because through the cumulative impact of actions taken by UNGA member-state delegations, committees, representatives, rapporteurs and leaders, all of which lend credence to the legitimacy of an era devoted to Israel's violent destruction, and therefore a large segment of the world's Jewish population. This has happened based on the weight of actions, policies, arguments and concerns that were applied toward South Africa during the long decades of its apartheid era. Often led by but by no means limited to Islamic Republic of Iran, the UNGA has thus socialized, authenticated and normalized broad anti-Israel public sentiment into the fabric of Western societies through an ever-expanding universe of claims and controversies relating to apartheid and racial discrimination. 

As one of six \enquote{principal organs} of the UN,\footnote{The UNGA is one of ``six principal organs of the United Nations, as established by the UN Charter.'' United Nations (n.d). Main Bodies. \url{https://www.un.org/en/about-us/main-bodies}.} the primary way the UNGA has defined and defended this narrative is by allowing hostile member states and their hirelings to use its forums and impact delivery vehicles to castigate Israel for having ``usurped'' on racist grounds the ``ancestral homeland'' of Palestine.\footnote{Kali Robinson (Last modified July 12, 2023), ``What Is U.S. Policy on the Israeli-Palestinian Conflict?,'' Council on Foreign Relations. \url{https://www.cfr.org/backgrounders/what-us-policy-israeli-palestinian-conflict}.} Essentially, such claims are merely elaborative echoes of the powerful slanders of Dr. Fayez Sayegh, an Arab diplomat whose career in the UNGA spanned the critical decades of this long process. His corrupt influence, aided by his immense charm and prolific pen, will be considered in some detail in section IV. But over 60 years ago, and during a brief interlude from his retainer on behalf of various Arab states, whether individually or as a collective, he made the case that still animates how the expanding network of Israel's most diehard enemies --- both within the UNGA and outside it --- still thinks \parencite[214]{Sayegh-1965}:

\begin{quote}
    Racism is not an acquired trait of the Zionist settler-state. Nor is it an accidental, passing feature of the Israeli scene. It is congenial, essential and permanent. For it is inherent in the very ideology of Zionism and in the basic motivation for Zionist colonisation and statehood.
\end{quote}

The singular impact of this assertion is clear not only from the UNGA's passage of its infamous \enquote{Zionism as Racism} resolution (3379) ten years later (1975),\footnote{Elimination of all forms of racial discrimination: Zionism as racism --- UNGA resolution 3379, on the report of the Third Committee (A/10320), November 11, 1975, Document A/RES/3379 XXX, \url{https://www.un.org/unispal/document/auto-insert-181963/}.} which basically reiterates it and which he both largely authored and tirelessly evangelized, but also the fact that it derives from a tradition of characterizing Israel --- again, using his words --- as having a ``vital link'' with western imperialism \parencite[21]{Sayegh-1965}. Because racism has come to be widely understood over the past half century as both cause and effect of imperialism's success, the view has become entrenched that Israel's capitulation is only the start of a struggle to end a multi-century era in which the world's ``national capitalist economies'' engineered ``the racialization of the peoples of the entire world'' \parencite[89]{Silva-1999}. The passage of Resolution 3379 gave Israel's adversaries the template for solidifying worldwide opposition in the name of an ``enlightened worldview adopted by good people.''\footnote{Deist, J. (2023). \textit{A Strange Liberty: Politics Drops Its Pretenses}. Ludwig von Mises Institute. \url{https://mises.org/online-book/strange-liberty-politics-drops-its-pretenses/15-getting-liberalism-wrong}. (``In this view, liberalism is a way of thinking, a kind of enlightened worldview adopted by good people. Liberalism becomes almost undefinable by its breadth: an interconnected passel of open-minded and tolerant attitudes across a swath of 'social' issues. Cosmopolitanism, reason, tolerance, feminism, antiracism, and a healthy degree of support for egalitarian democracy are at the fore; economics is not the focus.'')} This, in turn, resulted from a broad shift in academic understandings about the nature of race and racism that occurred during the same era that the UN became operational, with a key ingredient being \enquote{the emergence of a new transnational political culture during the post-war era, that also included other solidarity movements, as well as student's, green, peace and women's movements, often conceptualized as \enquote{the new social movements},} coupled with South African anti-apartheid activism then and Israeli anti-apartheid activism now, creating what has become \enquote{a `movement of movements'.}\footnote{Håkan Thörn, \textit{Anti-Apartheid and the Emergence of a Global Civil Society}, Palgrave: London, 2006, 8.} 

But Resolution 3379's passage did not just give the world a simplistic metaphor. It also reflected the core playbook of tactics used to make the metaphor stick, and make sense, not only to far too many UNGA member states with sinister motivations, but also --- and more emotionally and naively --- their civilian populations. Its preamble, for example, notes another conference of ``Non-Aligned Countries'' earlier that year, which ended with a statement that ``most severely condemned zionism as a threat to world peace and security.'' This conference, held in Lima, Peru, reflected the combined strength of post-colonial African and Asian states, most of which attended except those considered too deeply aligned with western powers (Turkey, Iran and Israel).\footnote{Resolutions of the Fifth Conference of Foreign Ministers of Non-Aligned Countries on the issue of Palestine and the Middle East, Lima, Peru, 1975, August 30, 1975, Baath Newspapers. \url{https://www.un.org/unispal/document/auto-insert-181963/}. 43 of the 94 countries represented at the Lima conference were in the MENA or sub-Saharan Africa regions. Non-attending MENA and African states were Israel, South Africa, Turkey and Iran. UNGA Seventh special session, Agenda item 7, DEVELOPMENT AND INTERNATIONAL ECONOMIC CO-OPERATION --- Letter dated 1 September 1975 from the Permanent Representative of Peru to the United Nations addressed to the Secretary-General, 5 September 1975, Documeent A/10217, downloadable from \url{https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/578291?ln=en&v=pdf}.} Together, they ensured Resolution 3379's comfortable passage.\footnote{The final tally was 72 countries in favor, 35 opposed and 35 abstentions.} Notably, however, while during that now distant era, ``issues of a regional, local or specifically national nature'' often predominated among these nations, still ``the active and indispensable role played by the UAR (Egypt)'' from the beginning ``imparted an anti-Israeli character to virtually every non-aligned conference'' \parencite[157]{Bandyopadhyaya-1977}. By the mid-1960s, this alliance became a foregone conclusion even though there was a chance --- at least with respect to questions pertaining to haggling over Israel's existence --- that it might have foundered, but for the intransigence of Nasser's Egypt and the geopolitical machinations of the Soviet Union.\footnote{Record of the Meeting Between Secretary of State Dulles and President Tito on the Island of Vanga, November 6, 1955, 3-5:40 p.m. 1 USDel/MC/24. Foreign Relations of the United States, 1955--1957, Central and Southeastern Europe, Volume XXVI, U.S. Office of the Historian. \url{https://history.state.gov/historicaldocuments/frus1955-57v26/d263} After noting that  \enquote{the present trouble [in the Middle East] was the Egyptian arms deal engineered by the Soviet Union,} the author of this memorandum noted in closing that while President Tito \enquote{personally thought well of Col. Nasser in that he was honest and well-intentioned, \ldots he [considered him] fanatical on the problem of Israel.}} 
 
The constellation of member-states that then defined a coherent basis for collective hostility toward Israel remains a dominant majority within the UNGA. It is no longer aided by the votes, strategic guidance and material assistance of the USSR and its ``eastern bloc'' satellites, but it remains vibrant with newly enlisted supporters, whether working inside its buildings or on its behalf in the field. As a result, notwithstanding the 1991 revocation of Resolution 3379, the widely-shared perception that Israel should not exist within the MENA region, or indeed anywhere in the world, has never truly deviated from the substance of Resolution 3379, or Sayegh's vitriol, or even the musings of an American agricultural development expert who was appointed UNWRA's first commissioner and was largely unfamiliar with the intricacies of the Arab or Jewish worlds.\footnote{John H. Davis. \textit{The evasive peace: a study of the Zionist-Arab problem}. John Murray: Lonson, 1968, 96. (``Since Israel does not fit into the Middle East, as it is constituted, and since she herself is powerless to alter her ways in order to fit in, it follows that it is futile to expect Israel and the Arab States to come to peace terms through direct negotiations and without assistance from a third party. In fact, the only way that, left to their own devices, they can ever arrive at peaceful terns is through a war in which one side eliminates the other as a political entity, or at least alters its nature in a manner which makes it compatible with the whole. The persistence with which hostilities have continued during the past twenty years and the intermittent wars that have erupted bear out the truth of this statement.'')} Israel remains the ``usurper, lacking even the semblance of legitimacy'' \parencite[49]{Sayegh-1965} and, while the USSR's heyday ended long ago, its torch has been effectively passed to new nation-state leadership (most notably, from Iran, Qatar, Pakistan and Turkey) as well as transnational and individual organizations and actors (literally and figuratively) within western realms, most notably its global humanitarian NGOs and elite faculty members and departments of the \enquote{progressive left} Academy.

Has this loosely assembled coterie of Israel's current protagonists finally succeeded in doing what its predecessors long dreamed of, in turning the tables on the Jewish state in their own societies and organizations, including many Jews? The answer almost feels like it is on the tip of the world's collective tongue, yet its substance still remains not entirely clear. Although \enquote{[t]he school that classifies Israel as an apartheid regime based on the analogy with South Africa} has only the flimsiest and most theoretically convoluted framework to defend its position when considered in the light of comparative politics,\footnote{Gal Ariely,\enquote{Israel's Regime Untangled --- Between Democracy and Apartheid,} Cambridge University Press: Cambridge and New York, 2011), 26} the comparison is yet peddled pervasively and seemingly prevails. Israel's endangerment due to the visceral effect of their abstractions over racism now spill beyond its regional neighborhood into the lands of her supporters everywhere. The grim comeuppance of its redoubtable network of defenders at last seems at hand. As a Turkish official informed visiting dignitaries in a 2025 meeting on ``the ongoing humanitarian crisis in Gaza,'' the world now has an \enquote{Israel problem} because Israel has trampled on international law and \enquote{\enquote*{fed on chaos.}}\footnote{  Daily Sabah with AA, \enquote{The world has an `Israel problem': Turkish Communications Director}. \textit{Daily Sabah}, September 17, 2025. \url{https://www.dailysabah.com/politics/the-world-has-an-israel-problem-turkish-communications-director/news}.} But there is yet time to consider how such claims are in fact bred to foment their own chaos, largely delivered through the actions and edicts of the UNGA and other weighty forces acting within civil society, and are best understood as being made ``in a mirror.''\footnote{The phrase ``accusation in a mirror'' describes the technique of projection by which propagandists ``impute to enemies exactly what they and their own party are planning to do.'' Kenneth Marcus (2012) ``Accusation in a Mirror.'' \textit{Loyola Univ. Chi. Law Journal}, 43,2, 357--393. \url{http://lawecommons.luc.edu/luclj/vol43/iss2/5}.}
